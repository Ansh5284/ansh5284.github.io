Cost Control and Cost Reduction

Cost Control implies regulation of the cost of operation through setting targets and continuously comparing actual performance against them. It's about maintaining costs within established limits.

Cost Reduction is the real and permanent reduction in the unit cost of goods manufactured or service rendered without impairing the utility for the intended use. It's a continuous effort to reduce costs to an optimal level through various means like standardization, improved design, and efficient operations.

Key Differences:

Feature Cost Reduction Cost Control
Nature Real & Permanent Reduction Comparison to Standards/Budgets
Savings Realistic & Permanent Could be Temporary
Quality/Utility Retained Not Guaranteed
Approach Dynamic, Challenges Standards Less Dynamic, Complies with Standards
Focus Present & Future Costs Present & Past Behaviour

Target Costing

Target costing is a structured approach to determining the cost at which a proposed product with specified functionality and quality must be produced, to generate a desired level of profitability at its anticipated selling price. It's a market-driven approach, starting with the customer's willingness to pay.

Key Steps:

  1. Re-orient culture to market-driven prices and customer needs.
  2. Identify market requirements (design, utility, need).
  3. Establish market-driven target price.
  4. Determine target cost (Target Price - Desired Profit Margin).
  5. Establish the target costing process (roles, responsibilities, tools).
  6. Brainstorm and analyze alternatives for cost reduction.
  7. Use tools (e.g., Value Engineering/Analysis) to close the gap between current cost and target cost.
  8. Measure results and maintain focus on continuous improvement.

Value Analysis (VA) & Value Engineering (VE):

Value Analysis (VA) is a scientific approach to cost reduction for *existing* products, reviewing material composition and production design without reducing customer value.

Value Engineering (VE) is the application of value analysis to *new* products, focusing on cost avoidance during the design phase. Both involve a complete audit of the product.

Issues addressed in VA/VE:

  • Eliminate non-value-added functions from the production process.
  • Eliminate excessive durability or reliability.
  • Minimize the design (fewer parts/features).
  • Design the product better for the manufacturing process (Design for Manufacture and Assembly).
  • Substitute parts with less expensive components/materials.
  • Combine steps (process centering).
  • Take supplier's assistance for technology and cost reduction.
  • Seek a "better way" (radical re-thinking).

Life Cycle Costing

Life cycle costing is a system that identifies and accumulates the actual costs and corresponding revenues, attributable to a cost object, from its inception to abandonment. It aims to maximize the profit a product generates over its entire life, considering all stages.

Product Life Cycle Stages:

  • Introduction: New product launch, low awareness, minimal competition, negative or low profits. Focus on awareness and trial.
  • Growth: Rapid sales expansion, increasing competition, profits may start declining towards the end. Focus on brand identity, quality, and distribution.
  • Maturity: Sales increase at a decreasing rate, intense price competition, profits decline. Focus on strong marketing, product differentiation, and price adjustments.
  • Decline: Sales volume drops, demand disappears due to substitutes or technological advances, profits decline or become negative. Strategies include maintaining for niche, discontinuing, or using as a replacement product.

Benefits:

  • Earlier actions for revenue generation/cost reduction.
  • More accurate assessment of revenues and costs.
  • Promotes long-term rewarding over short-term.
  • Framework for total incremental costs over product life.
  • Provides long-term profitability picture and feedback.
  • Enhances control of manufacturing costs.
  • Traces R&D and development costs to individual products.

Theory of Constraints (TOC)

TOC focuses on revenue and cost management when faced with bottlenecks, which restrict output. It advocates full utilization of bottleneck resources and avoiding 100% utilization of non-bottleneck resources to prevent inventory build-up.

Operational Measures:

  • Throughput (T): Rate of generating money through sales. $T = \text{(Sales Revenue – Unit Level Variable Expenses)} / \text{Time}$. (Direct Labor is fixed).
  • Investment (I): Money tied up in the system (assets like facilities, equipment).
  • Operating Expense (OE): Money spent to convert investment into throughput (direct labor, all operating/maintenance expenses).

Management objectives: Increase Throughput, Minimize Investment, Decrease Operating Expenses.

Goldratt’s Five-Step Method:

  1. Identify the System Bottlenecks: Find constraints limiting output.
  2. Exploit the Bottlenecks: Fully utilize the bottleneck activity.
  3. Subordinate Everything Else: Non-bottleneck activities should align their pace with the bottleneck.
  4. Elevate the System Bottlenecks: Take action to remove or increase the bottleneck's efficiency/capacity.
  5. Repeat the Process: If a new constraint emerges, return to step 1.

Throughput Accounting (TA)

Developed from the Theory of Constraints, Throughput Accounting monitors the rate at which businesses make money. It focuses on return per product per bottleneck hour, treating only direct material as variable and all labor and overhead costs as fixed.

Throughput Accounting Ratio:

$\text{TA Ratio} = \frac{\text{Throughput per Bottleneck Minute}}{\text{Factory Cost per Bottleneck Minute}}$

A TA ratio greater than 1 indicates a profitable product, as throughput generated exceeds total factory cost.

Advantages:

  • Reduction in inventory.
  • More productive machines.
  • Ability to meet shorter lead times.
  • More flexible.
  • Better customer service and product mix.

Disadvantages:

  • Focus on short-term goals.
  • Main emphasis on increasing sales and volume, not quality.
  • Might result in loss of the overall picture.
  • Focuses on push approach (vs. JIT's pull).
  • Validity depends on application to total supply chain.
  • Simplistic view of operating expenses as fixed.

Environmental Management Accounting (EMA)

EMA is the process of collecting and analyzing information related to environmental costs for internal decision-making. It identifies and estimates costs of environment-related activities and seeks to control them, considering both financial and environmental costs/benefits.

Why EMA is Gaining Importance:

  • Environmental costs can be a significant proportion of operating costs.
  • Increasing regulatory requirements and severe consequences for non-compliance.
  • Society prefers environmentally responsible corporates.

Environmental Cost Classifications (Hansen & Mendoza):

  • Environmental Prevention Costs: Preventing adverse environmental impacts (e.g., pollution control equipment, environmental policies).
  • Environmental Appraisal Costs: Determining compliance with environmental standards (e.g., monitoring, testing, reporting).
  • Environmental Internal Failure Costs: Costs from activities produced but not discharged into the environment (e.g., recycling scrap, decommissioning).
  • Environmental External Failure Costs: Costs incurred after discharging waste into the environment (e.g., cleaning up contaminated soil, restoring land).

EMA Methodologies for Identification & Allocation:

  • Input-Output Analysis: Balances material inflows with outflows (productive output vs. waste).
  • Flow Cost Accounting: Considers organizational structure and material losses at various stages, recording quantity and value.
  • Life Cycle Costing: Accounts for costs and revenues over a product's entire life, including full environmental cost.
  • Activity Based Costing (ABC): Allocates environmental costs (both direct and hidden in overheads) to cost centers/drivers based on environmental impact.
