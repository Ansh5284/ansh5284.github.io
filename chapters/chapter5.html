Management of Cost Strategically for Emerging Business Models

This chapter focuses on the underlying elements driving changes in the business environment, how these changes give rise to new business models, and how organizations are adapting. It discusses various business models relevant to hyper-disruptive environments, sustainability, and emerging markets, and suggests strategic responses to them.

Changing Business Environment: Change Drivers

The business environment is dynamic, forcing leaders to assess and respond to changes driven by technology, competition, sustainability, and innovation. These "change drivers" determine the degree of environmental dynamism. Some are causes (e.g., digital technologies), while others are effects (e.g., advanced manufacturing).

Digital Technologies

Digital technologies are electronic tools, systems, devices, and resources that generate, store, or process data, underpinned by network and internet advancements. They have rapidly transformed societies by enhancing connectivity and access to services.

Key Technologies:

  • Internet of Things (IoT): Continuous connectivity of smart devices and sensors for insights and analytics.
  • Robotics: Technologies sensing input, applying AI, and reacting physically for repetitive and analytical tasks.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI): Technologies resembling human thought, releasing unattainable analytics and insights.
  • Automation: Technology performing work traditionally done by humans, minimizing human input.
  • Cloud: Delivers computer infrastructure and software services over a network, enabling flexibility and scalability.
  • Autonomous Vehicles: Vehicles navigating and driving without human operators.
  • 3D Printing: Additive manufacturing building objects layer by layer from design files.
  • Digital Twin: Virtual representations of physical counterparts (products, processes, tasks) for comprehension and prediction.
  • Augmented Reality: Seamlessly connects physical, digital, and human worlds, providing real-time visual information.
  • Mobile Internet: Enables continuous connectivity of computing and smart devices, foundational for other technologies.
  • Blockchain: System recording public transactions in multiple records (chains) for secure, immutable digital ledgers.

Technological Advancement Classifications:

  • **Automation:** Mere replacement of humans with technology (e.g., passbook update kiosk).
  • **Extension:** Using technology for new tasks that enhance product/service utility (e.g., mobile banking).
  • **Transformation:** Use of technology that revamps product/services, processes, and culture (e.g., ATMs, digital clearing).

Business Ecosystems

A business ecosystem is a network of organizations (suppliers, distributors, customers, competitors, government) involved in delivering a product/service through competition and cooperation. It's about co-existence and co-evolution through ongoing interactions.

Competitive advantage shifts from 'doing better' individually to how partnerships help all involved parties 'do better' collectively, creating more value than they could alone.

Three Mutually Reinforcing Flywheels for Ecosystem Success:

  • **Data Flywheel:** Learning effect from more and richer data providing deeper insights.
  • **Growth Flywheel:** Network effects from more users and partners, offering improved value propositions.
  • **Cost Flywheel:** Scale effects by spreading fixed costs, lowering unit costs, and improving value proposition.

Hyper Competition

Hyper-competition is intense market instability where competitive advantages are temporary. Buyers have strong bargaining power, demanding higher quality and cheaper products, forcing businesses to constantly change strategies.

Characteristics:

  • High rivalry, quick and unexpected strategic maneuvers.
  • Rapid technological and structural changes, flexible strategies.
  • Low entry barriers, diminishing geographic/industrial barriers.
  • Significant global alliances among competitors.
  • Strong buyer bargaining power with fragmented preferences.
  • Competitive advantage is temporary; new competitiveness replaces old.

Response (Strategy):

D'Aveni's 7S framework (Stakeholder's satisfaction, Strategic soothsaying, Speed, Surprise, Signals, Shifting the rules of a market, Simultaneous and sequential thrust) guides organizations in high-velocity markets to deliver temporary advantages.

Transformation and Disruption

Innovations can gradually evolve industries or cause significant shifts. **Transformation** occurs when an innovation shifts the basis of competition (e.g., UPI transforming small merchant payments). **Disruption** occurs when a challenger offers greater value in a way existing firms cannot compete directly (e.g., app-based taxi services).

Types of Disruptive Innovation (Clayton Christensen):

  • **Low-end disruption:** Company uses a low-cost model to enter the bottom of an existing market, targeting overserved customers (e.g., online booksellers).
  • **New-market disruption:** New entrant expands the market by targeting customers who have never used a similar product, making it more accessible or less expensive (e.g., early personal computers).

Components of Disruptive Innovation:

  • **Enabling Technology:** Innovation requires the ability to create a better product.
  • **Disruptive or Innovative Business Model:** Targets new or low-end customers.
  • **Coherent Value Network:** Must be accepted across a coherent value network, including suppliers.

Advanced Manufacturing

Advanced manufacturing uses innovative technologies and methodologies to enhance competitiveness. It aims to enhance output, increase added value, quality, responsiveness, and flexibility, while reducing time to market, unit quantities, material content, and inventory, and removing underutilized capital plant.

Levels of Integration (Meredith and Hill):

  1. **Stand-alone (or unitary):** Equipment like robots or NC machine tools.
  2. **Cells:** Groups of equipment and materials for part production, utilizing group technology (GT) and CAM, potentially forming Flexible Manufacturing Systems (FIMS).
  3. **Linked islands:** Cells from level 2 linked into larger production systems (CAD/CAM, automated storage/retrieval, JIT, MRP II).
  4. **Full integration:** Linkage of the entire manufacturing function through an extensive information network (Computer Integrated Manufacturing - CIM).

Lean Start-up

Lean Start-up is a framework for new businesses to test, learn, and adjust their strategy through continuous improvement, rather than creating elaborate business plans. It helps entrepreneurs navigate uncertainties by testing hypotheses with a Minimum Viable Product (MVP).

Traditional vs. Lean Start-up:

Basis Traditional Lean Start-up
Strategy Business Plan & Implementation Business Model & Hypothesis
New-Product Process Product Management Customer Development
Engineering Agile or Waterfall Agile (Iterative & Incremental)
Organisation Department by function Customer & Agile Dev Teams
Reporting Accounting (Financial) Metrics that matter (CAC, LTV)

Methodology:

The core is the **build-measure-learn feedback loop**. Identify a problem, create an MVP, measure its impact, and learn from data. If needed, pivot to a new fundamental hypothesis.

Agile Organisations

An agile organization's structure, policies, and capabilities enable employees to respond quickly to changing environments and customer needs. It's built on a network of empowered teams with high alignment, accountability, expertise, transparency, and collaboration.

Characteristics:

  • No traditional command structure or top-down hierarchy.
  • Customer-centric approach.
  • Shared goal and vision with open communication.
  • Employees comfortable sharing best practices, open to new experiences, quick decision-makers.
  • Proactive employee development and emotional intelligence valued.
  • Strong growth mindset, continuously improving efficiency and impact.

Innovation Core:

Innovation happens when **Design Thinking**, **Lean Start-up**, and **Agile** methodologies combine, bringing creative solutions and technical capabilities together early in the process.

Start-ups vs. Incumbents

**Startups** are temporary organizations designed to find a repeatable and scalable business model, willing to take risks and cause disruption. **Incumbents** are permanent, established organizations that execute repeatable and scalable business models, leading the market with established brands and audiences.

The battle often comes down to whether the startup gains distribution before the incumbent innovates. Incumbents have assets like customers, brand value, and systems, but often lack the fluidity, flexibility, speed, and agility of startups.

Key Differences:

Basis Start-up Incumbents
Organization Structure Lean, cross-functional, empowered teams, shared responsibility. Hierarchical, functional departments, siloed, slow decision-making.
Culture & People Risk-taking, market-capturing, dynamic, entrepreneurial. Risk-averse, market-defending, bureaucratic, process-driven.
Operating Model Small production, online sales, no set processes, digital communication. Minimum viable market size, offline sales, stable processes, traditional media.
Consumer & Innovation Offer what consumers want, new niche categories, fast turnaround. Offer what they're good at, adjacent categories, slower turnaround.
Financing Seldom profitable initially, answerable to investors. Strong financial position, high internal capability, answerable to shareholders.

Intrapreneurship

Intrapreneurship is entrepreneurship within an existing business, allowing individuals (intrapreneurs) to pursue opportunities independently of current resources. It's a revolutionary system for speeding up innovations in large companies by leveraging entrepreneurial talent and countering stagnation.

Intrapreneurs are 'dreamers who do', carrying high vision and involvement in execution, even though they don't have ownership of the new venture. Organizations should ideally view intrapreneurial teams as profit-centers for sustainability and cost efficiency.

Innovation Hubs and Incubators

An **innovation hub** is a physical space for researchers, creators, and innovators to nurture ideas. **Incubators** (also called startup hubs) focus on early-stage startups without a business model, guiding them to develop viable products through structured programs (seed funding, training, networking). They typically work on a fee-basis.

**Accelerators** differ by focusing on rapid growth for startups that already have an MVP, providing mentorship and industry connections over a short-term program.

Supply Chain Partnerships

Supply chain partnership is about coordinating with internal departments and external partners for optimized flow, on-time delivery, and resilience. It increases agility, reduces bullwhip effects, and ensures free flow of information.

Approaches to Supply Chain Innovations:

  • **Rapid supply chains:** Prioritize speed and efficiency.
  • **Agile supply chains:** Emphasize responsiveness to demand changes (volume and variety).
  • **Lean supply chains:** Prioritize waste elimination (including time).

Essentials of Seamless Collaboration (rely on IT):

  • Real-time data sharing (EDI, bar coding, RFIDs, POS, EFT).
  • Configurable workflows for system alignment.
  • AI and Machine Learning for faster threat identification and recommendations.
  • In-Context Messaging for real-time communication.
  • End-to-end visibility for identifying potential problems early.

Emerging Business Models Overview

In a disruptive environment, generic business models may fail, leading to the emergence of new models that create and capture value in novel ways. These models are often categorized by their relevance to hyper-disruptive environments, sustainability, and emerging markets.

Categories:

  • Hyper Disruptive Business Models
  • Models Relevant to Sustainability
  • Models Relevant to Emerging National Markets

Hyper Disruptive Business Models

These models create, disintermediate, refine, reengineer, or optimize products/services, roles, or markets. They prioritize crushing competitors and staying at the industry's top by achieving distinct competitive advantages.

Prominent Models:

  • **The Free Model:** Core product free to many, premium products sold to a subset (e.g., social media platforms). Sub-categories: Advertising (Hidden Revenue), Cross-subsidisation (Razorblade), Open Source (Free Access), Promotion.
  • **The Subscription Model:** Consumers pay a recurring fee for access to a service or product (e.g., OTT platforms, newspapers).
  • **Freemium Model:** Basic services free (users pay with data), fee for comprehensive offer/upgraded version (e.g., professional community platforms, SaaS).
  • **The Digital Platform Model (E-Commerce):** Provides a digital marketplace for buyers and sellers, earning fees/commissions. Sub-categories: B2B, B2C, C2C, C2B.
  • **The Hypermarket Model:** Offers products/services at lower cost prices, leveraging economies of scale (brick-mortar, click, or hybrid).
  • **The Access-Over-Ownership Model:** Provides temporary access to goods/services traditionally purchased (e.g., vehicle rental apps, shared lounges).
  • **The Service Ecosystem Model:** Integrated products where customers are locked into using one facet, limiting competition (e.g., premium brands with integrated product ecosystems).
  • **The Experience Model:** Offers unique, stylish, innovative products with an added experience component, elevating value and making it hard for customers to switch (e.g., luxury car manufacturer plant visits).
  • **On Demand Model:** Monetizes time, charging a premium for instant access, providing convenience (e.g., app-based transport, online grocery).

Models Relevant to Sustainability

Sustainable business models address social and environmental perspectives in addition to economic profitability. They incorporate value propositions with positive social and environmental offerings.

Three Dimensions of Sustainability:

  • **Social:** Philanthropy, ethics, inclusion, diversity.
  • **Environmental:** Carbon footprint, resource depletion.
  • **Economic:** Revenue, profitability.

Approaches for Developing Sustainable Business Models:

  • **Product Service Systems:** Consumer pays for services of a product, not ownership (company responsible for full lifecycle).
  • **Open Innovation:** Collaboration with external organizations/individuals to generate and commercialize new ideas.
  • **Peer-to-peer innovation:** Collaboration of loosely connected individuals sharing open-source resources.
  • **Closed-loop production:** Recycling materials throughout manufacturing, minimizing waste ('cradle to cradle').
  • **Crowdfunding:** New funding source for niche innovation, attracting social media attention.
  • **Sharing economy:** Participatory sharing schemes providing timely access to resources, efficient use of idle assets.
  • **Social enterprises and benefit corporations:** Legally obligated to pursue social and environmental value alongside financial.
  • **The gift economy:** Voluntary donations ('pay what you want'), capitalizing on social sustainability.
  • **New manufacturing paradigm:** Driven by additive manufacturing (3D printing) for efficiency, rapid prototyping.

Models Relevant to Emerging National Markets

Emerging markets (e.g., India, China) are transitioning societies with rapid informatization, limited industrialization, and growing middle classes. They represent substantial global consumption opportunities but are often price-sensitive, requiring customized business models.

Characteristics of Emerging Markets (require customization in business models):

  • Sociopolitical governance.
  • Market heterogeneity.
  • Inadequate infrastructure.
  • Chronic shortage of resources.
  • Unbranded competition.
  • Prevalence of clientelistic exchange.
  • Informal institutional flux.
  • Channels of distribution challenges.

Businesses targeting these markets must often rethink their models from scratch to create and capture value in new ways, acting like startups.

Strategic Responses to New Business Models

Successful business models require strategic responses to change drivers, especially root causes like digital technologies, business ecosystems, hyper-competition, and transformation/disruption. These cause drivers lead to new business models, while effect drivers are responses themselves.

Strategies Pertaining to Digital Technologies:

For startups, digital technologies are core to innovation and disruption. For incumbents, it's balancing optimizing existing products/markets with striving for innovation for sustainable growth.

Value-Based Strategy in a Business Ecosystem:

Strategy should dominate aspects of the ecosystem that create significant customer value. Strategic decisions involve investments in resources and capabilities to capture value.

  • Make the best out of changing customer needs.
  • Be the locus of USP (reason customers buy the product).
  • Be an indispensable integrator (e.g., app stores).

Strategic Aspects of Creating Ecosystem (Partnerships):

  • Flexibility and cooperation with partners.
  • Enhanced accountability (partners as stakeholders).
  • Free (seamless) flow of information (transparency vs. trade secrets).
  • Shared economy (clustering of firms, shared resources).
  • Think of economics of scope apart from scale.

Focus on Series of Short-Term Advantages (Hyper-Competition):

Continuously strive for short-term advantages through market disruption, moving to new disruptions as old ones mature. Requires constant innovation, R&D budget, lean start-up structure, and collaboration.

Strategic Response to Transformations and Disruptions:

Disruptions change the basis of competition. Failing to respond makes established organizations vulnerable. Strategies include:

  • **Milking as cash cow:** For vulnerable businesses, winding-up as last resort.
  • **Invest or counter invest:** Strive for or respond to disruption with incremental investments.
  • **Blocking the path or creating hurdle:** Innovators reserve IP rights; peers create hurdles.
  • **Counter disruption:** Aggressive strategy where large players introduce another disruption.
  • **Restrict presence and shift focus on core:** Redefine core capabilities and shift focus.
  • **Withdraw:** Exit strategy, liquidating assets and diverting value to profitable businesses.

Considerations for strategy selection: availability of resources, importance of segment, organizational capabilities, and trade-off between first-mover advantage and uncertainty.
