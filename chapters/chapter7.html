Strategic Profit Management

This chapter focuses on strategic profitability analysis, advanced activity-based costing (ABC), activity-based management (ABM), and activity-based budgeting (ABB). It also covers Pareto analysis as a tool for prioritizing efforts in profit management.

Strategic Profitability Analysis

Operating profit is affected by changes in revenue and costs. Strategic analysis of operating profit breaks down changes into three components: Growth, Price Recovery, and Productivity. This analysis covers both revenue and cost effects.

1. Growth Component:

Measures change in operating income due to change in quantity of output sold.

Revenue Effect of Growth Formula:

$\text{Revenue Effect of Growth} = [(\text{Actual units of output sold in current year}) - (\text{Actual units of output sold in last year})] \times \text{Selling price in last year}$

Cost Effect of Growth for Variable Costs Formula:

$\text{Cost Effect of Growth for Variable Costs} = [(\text{Unit of input required to produce current year output in last year}) - (\text{Actual units of input used to produce last year's output})] \times \text{Input price in last year}$

Cost Effect of Growth for Fixed Costs Formula:

$\text{Cost Effect of Growth for Fixed Costs} = [(\text{Actual units of capacity in last year if adequate to produce current year's production in last year}) - (\text{Actual units of capacity in last year})] \times \text{Price per unit of capacity in last year}$

2. Price Recovery Component:

Measures changes in operating income solely due to changes in prices.

Revenue Effect of Price Recovery Formula:

$\text{Revenue Effect of Price Recovery} = [(\text{Selling price in current year}) - (\text{Selling price in last year})] \times \text{Actual units of output sold in current year}$

Cost Effect of Price Recovery for Variable Cost Formula:

$\text{Cost Effect of Price Recovery for Variable Cost} = [(\text{Input price in current year}) - (\text{Input price in last year})] \times \text{Units of input required to produce current year's output in last year}$

Cost Effect of Price Recovery for Fixed Costs Formula:

$\text{Cost Effect of Price Recovery for Fixed Costs} = [(\text{Price per unit of capacity in current year}) - (\text{Price per unit of capacity in last year})] \times \text{Actual units of capacity in last year, if adequate to produce current year's output in last year}$

3. Productivity Component:

Measures change in operating income due to changes in product mix and/or yield of inputs, using current year's input prices.

Cost Effect of Productivity for Variable Cost Formula:

$\text{Cost Effect of Productivity for Variable Cost} = [(\text{Actual units of input used to produce current year's output}) - (\text{Units of input required to produce current year's output in last year})] \times \text{Input price in current year}$

Cost Effect of Productivity for Fixed Costs Formula:

$\text{Cost Effect of Productivity for Fixed Costs} = [(\text{Actual units of capacity in current year}) - (\text{Actual units of capacity in last year if adequate to produce current year's production in last year})] \times \text{Price per unit of capacity in current year}$

Profitability Analysis Through Activity Based Costing (ABC)

ABC is a methodology for costing activities, resources, and cost objects, aiding management evaluation and decision-making. It's especially useful in service sectors where overheads are not volume-related.

1. Direct Product Profitability (DPP):

Measures the profitability of an individual product, attributing purchase price and other indirect costs (distribution, warehousing, retailing) to each product line. It provides a net profit, not just gross profit, for each product.

Benefits of DPP:

  • Better cost analysis.
  • Better pricing decisions.
  • Better management of stores and warehouse space.
  • Rationalization of product ranges.

DPP Statement Structure:

Sales

Less: Cost of Goods Sold

Gross Margin

Less: Direct Product Costs (Warehouse, Transportation, Store, etc.)

Direct Product Profit

Indirect costs for DPP are analyzed into: Overhead Cost, Volume Related Cost, Product Batch Cost, and Inventory Financing Costs.

2. Customer Profitability Analysis (CPA):

Costs customers to determine their relative profitability. Different customers use activities differently, allowing for customer profitability profiles. This helps identify which customers contribute to or erode overall profitability and provides a basis for dialogue to improve margins.

ABC, ABM, ABB

1. ABC in Advanced Manufacturing Environment:

ABC helps bridge the gap in cost management due to global competitiveness and workplace automation. It provides accurate product costing by accounting for Non-unit Level Overhead Costs (Setup Cost, Inspection Cost, Material Handling Cost) that traditional volume-based systems pool as fixed costs.

2. Activity Based Cost Management (ABM):

Describes the cost management application of ABC. ABM focuses on efficient and effective management of activities to continuously improve customer value and profitability. It utilizes ABC information through cost driver analysis, activity analysis, and performance measurement.

Key Aspects of ABM:

  • **Cost Driver Analysis:** Identifying factors that cause changes in activity costs.
  • **Activity Analysis:** Eliminating non-value-added activities (NVA) and grouping activities into cost pools. NVA activities create waste, delays, and add costs without improving quality or function.
  • **Performance Analysis:** Focusing on activities with the most potential for improvement, based on expected cost (activity rates vs. benchmarks) and time spent (quality, cycle time, productivity).

Non-Value-Added (NVA) Activities (Wasteful):

  • **Storing:** Time/resources holding inventory.
  • **Moving:** Time/resources to move materials/WIP/finished goods.
  • **Waiting:** Time/resources waiting for next process.
  • **Inspecting:** Time/resources ensuring product meets specifications.
  • **Scheduling:** Time/resources determining production access/setups.

Manufacturing Cycle Efficiency (MCE) Formula:

$\text{MCE} = \frac{\text{Processing Time}}{\text{Manufacturing Cycle Time}}$

Where $\text{Manufacturing Cycle Time} = \text{Processing Time} + \text{Inspection Time} + \text{Waiting Time} + \text{Move Time} + \text{Queue Time}$

MCE measures the ratio of value-added time to total manufacturing time. A perfect MCE is 1, meaning all time is value-added. MCE helps reduce NVA activities, speeding up delivery and lowering costs.

3. Activity Based Budgeting (ABB):

A process of planning and controlling expected activities to derive a cost-effective budget that meets forecast workload and strategic goals. It's a reversal of ABC, analyzing products/services, required activities, and resources needed. ABB enhances financial forecast accuracy and management understanding.

Key Elements of ABB:

  • Type of work/activity to be performed.
  • Quantity of work/activity to be performed.
  • Cost of work/activity to be performed.

Pareto Analysis

Based on the '80:20 rule' (Pareto Principle), which states that approximately 80% of results come from 20% of effort (vital few), or 20% of sources cause 80% of problems. It helps prioritize resource application to causes with higher significance.

Benefits of Pareto Analysis:

  • Breaks down big problems into smaller pieces.
  • Identifies significance of each cause/factor.
  • Prioritizes where to focus and apply efforts.
  • Ensures optimal use of scarce resources.
  • Acts as a control mechanism.

Applications:

  • **Pricing of a Product:** Focus on 20% of products contributing 80% of revenue.
  • **Customer Profitability Analysis:** Identify 20% of customers generating 80% of profit.
  • **ABC Analysis - Stock Control:** Focus on 20% of stock items accounting for 80% of value.
  • **Application in Activity Based Costing:** 20% of cost drivers responsible for 80% of total cost pool.
  • **Quality Control:** Identify "vital few" causes responsible for most reported problems.
