Preparation of Performance Reports

This chapter explains the role of performance reports in the performance management system, particularly in the context of responsibility accounting. It covers aspects involved in preparing different types of performance reports, relevant external reporting frameworks, and how to analyze and evaluate performance using these reports.

Responsibility Accounting

Responsibility accounting involves collecting, summarizing, and reporting information where individual managers are held accountable for specific costs, revenue, or assets. It forms the foundation as an information sub-system of the Performance Management System for both for-profit and not-for-profit organizations.

It is directly linked to organizational structure, ensuring that managers are judged by how well they manage items under their control. It is apt where top management delegates decision-making authority.

Performance Reports and Their Role

A **performance report** is a document detailing the outcome of an activity or an individual's work, often comparing actuals to budget/standard and highlighting variances. The process is called **performance reporting**.

**Role of Performance Reports:**

  • Inform responsibility center managers of budget deviations, prompting corrective action.
  • Show effects of activity changes and management control.
  • Summarize operations from lower to upper management levels.
  • Evaluate manager performance at all operating levels.

Why entities issue and use performance reports:

  • Focus attention on issues causing poor performance.
  • Quantify and prioritize issues for immediate attention.
  • Foster communication and discussion of highlighted issues.
  • Benchmarking, resource planning, enhancing business performance, smooth communication, preparing external reports.

Aspects Involved in the Preparation of Performance Reports

The preparation process involves common aspects/steps:

  1. **Clear idea about user need/use-case:** Understand the audience (senior management, lower levels, executives) to tailor information and detail.
  2. **Establishment of Objectives:** Identify CSFs and KPIs in light of organizational objectives (vision/mission).
  3. **Add an Executive Summary:** Provide an overview/snapshot of performance information.
  4. **The Real Stuff - Performance Assessment:** Actual assessment of performance, evaluating major KPIs and metrics for each reporting segment. Includes transverse (cross-sectional) and longitudinal (time series) analysis.
  5. **Layout is Key - Visual Elements and Narrative Commentary:** Use tables, charts, graphs, and narrative to assist understanding and highlight important issues.
  6. **Details quoted shall be cross-checked:** Ensure reliability and quality; proof-reading for external reports.

Types of Information in Performance Reports:

  • **Financial:** Sales, profits, costs, GP%, ROI.
  • **Non-Financial Quantitative:** Product rejects %, sales volume, number of complaints.
  • **Non-Financial Qualitative:** Changes in reputation, effectiveness, customer satisfaction, staff morale.

Non-financial information is critical for long-term success; short-term financial gains from reducing quality/innovation can lead to long-term suffering. Qualitative information is important but harder to quantify (a 'construct').

Types of Performance Reports

Various types of reports are used to record and report performance-related data:

  • **Earned Value Report:** Integrates scope, schedule, and cost performance using earned value management.
  • **Forecasting Report:** Estimates future performance for resource utilization.
  • **Progress Report:** Reports what has been completed or developed since the last report.
  • **Status Report:** Captures the state of an activity/project at a particular point (snapshot).
  • **Trend Report:** Compares performance against the same period in previous reports (monthly, quarterly, annually).
  • **Variance Report:** Charts the difference between actual vs. planned progress.

External Reporting Frameworks Relevant to Strategic Performance Management

Beyond traditional financial reporting, sustainability-oriented frameworks are relevant:

  • **Triple Bottom Line (TBL):** Expands scope to include environmental (Planet) and social (People) impact alongside financial (Profit).
  • **Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) Report:** Based on GRI Guidelines for globally acceptable sustainability reporting standards.
  • **ESG Reporting:** Disclosure of environmental, social, and corporate governance data for investor transparency and peer inspiration.
  • **Integrated Report ():** Framework by IIRC providing a holistic view of value creation (short, medium, long term) using six capitals (financial, manufactured, human, natural, intellectual, social & relationship).

Analyze Performance Reports to Take Required Action

After reporting, the next step is to analyze information and take corrective/preventive action. This involves:

  • Setting a time frame for analysis (month, quarter, annual) to identify trends.
  • Identifying and documenting trends, focusing on aspects critical to the analysis aim.
  • Summarizing findings and creating an action plan for performance improvement.

This task typically rests with senior managers or business intelligence experts.
