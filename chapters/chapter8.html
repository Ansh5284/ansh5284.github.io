An Introduction to Strategic Performance Management

This chapter introduces the relevance of performance management for strategic decision-making. It analyzes the role of performance management systems in business integration using models like the value chain and McKinsey's 7S, and evaluates how organizational structure, culture, and strategy influence performance measurement. It also covers problems in planning, controlling, and measuring performance in complex business structures, behavioral issues, and predicting/preventing corporate failure.

Performance Management and its Link to Strategy

**Strategy** is a pattern of activities to achieve organizational objectives and adapt to environmental changes long-term (Drucker), or a competitive position by choosing a different set of activities to deliver unique value (Porter).

**Performance Management** involves determining organizational structure, establishing responsibility centers, setting performance measurement systems, fixing yardsticks, and reviewing performance to identify improvement areas.

Performance management systems provide relevant and reliable information for strategic decision-making, planning, and control. Performance is measured against Critical Success Factors (CSFs) and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) linked to strategy, aligning individual and organizational goals.

Models Linking Performance Management and Strategy:

  • **Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton):** Translates strategy into specific, measurable objectives across financial, customer, internal business processes, and learning & growth perspectives.
  • **Performance Pyramid (Cross & Lynch):** Integrates strategic objectives with operational performance dimensions across four levels (Corporate Vision, Business Units, Business Operating Systems, Departments/Workcenters), considering internal efficiency and external effectiveness.

Role of Performance Management in Business Integration

**Business Integration** brings all aspects of business into alignment to achieve objectives efficiently. It links people, operations, strategy, and technology, improving performance and overcoming sub-optimization by viewing processes from an entity's perspective.

1. Value Chain Analysis:

The sequential chain of activities that leads to the delivery of the final product to the customer, depicting how value accumulates. It comprises primary activities (directly involved in transformation) and support activities (provide assistance).

Primary Activities:

  • **Inbound Logistics:** Receiving, storing, handling raw material inputs.
  • **Operations:** Transforming raw materials into finished goods/services.
  • **Outbound Logistics:** Storing, distributing, delivering finished goods.
  • **Marketing and Sales:** Market research, marketing mix.
  • **After Sales Service:** Installation, training, repair.

Support Activities:

  • Firm Infrastructure, Technology Development, Human Resources Management, Procurement.

Value Chain Analysis identifies Key Value Drivers (CSFs) to achieve competitive advantage by classifying activities into value-added and non-value-added, aiming to eliminate non-value-added activities (for cost leadership) and focus on value-added activities (for product differentiation).

Value-Added (VA) vs. Non-Value-Added (NVA) Activities:

  • **VA Activities:** Indispensable to complete the process, customers willing to pay.
  • **NVA Activities:** Not valued by customer, create waste, delays, add costs (e.g., storing, moving, waiting, inspecting, scheduling).

Using Value Chain for Competitive Advantage:

  • Classification of VA/NVA activities for strategic action.
  • Decision on outsourcing (both non-core NVA and value-added activities).
  • Comparison with competitors' value chains (process mapping, gap analysis, benchmarking).
  • Customer's perspective (next user as customer).
  • 'Value System' (wider integration beyond the firm's boundaries, encompassing suppliers' suppliers and customers' customers).

2. McKinsey's 7S Framework:

Maps a constellation of interrelated factors (Subsystems) influencing an organization's ability to change and attain objectives. Guides organizational change, strategy implementation, and understanding weaknesses. No hierarchy; Shared Values are central.

Elements of 7S:

  • **Hard S (Easy to quantify/change):**
    • **Strategy:** Plan to achieve goals and competitive advantage.
    • **Structure:** Formal framework of job tasks, division, grouping, coordination.
    • **Systems:** Processes and daily activities (e.g., accounting, HR, MIS).
  • **Soft S (Difficult to describe/change, influenced by culture):**
    • **Style:** Informal rules, organizational culture, management style.
    • **Staff:** Human resources, intellect capital.
    • **Skills:** Core competencies of the organization.
    • **Shared Values:** Guiding beliefs that transcend segments.

Effective Use of 7S Framework:

  1. Start with Shared Values: Evaluate consistency with hard elements.
  2. Look at Hard Elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems): Assess mutual support.
  3. Look at Soft Elements (Style, Staff, Skills, Shared Values): Do they support desired hard elements?
  4. Review Changes: Iterative process of adjustment and re-analysis.

All elements must pull in the same direction for organizational effectiveness and greater business integration.

Influence of Organization Structure, Culture, and Strategy on Performance Measurement

An organization's structure, culture, and strategy significantly influence its performance and, consequently, the selection of performance measurement techniques and methods.

1. Influence of Structure:

Different structures (functional, divisional, matrix, project, network/virtual) require appropriate performance measurement systems. For example, in a matrix organization, individual performance is measured by both functional and project heads.

2. Influence of Culture:

Culture impacts the choice of performance measures and willingness to adopt new methods. Innovative cultures embrace new techniques (e.g., measuring innovation performance), while traditional cultures are more resistant.

3. Influence of Strategy:

Performance measurement techniques must align with the organization's strategy. Cost leadership strategies may use financial indicators (ROI, RI), while quality leadership strategies incorporate non-financial aspects (flexibility, lead time, skill level) alongside financial ones.

Strategic Performance Issues in Complex Business Structures

Complex business structures (e.g., virtual/network organizations, strategic alliances, joint ventures, franchising, licensing, multinationals, complex supply chains) have evolved for lower costs and greater specialization, but pose challenges for performance management.

Problems and Solutions:

  • **Establishing Objectives:** Difficult due to differing values, vision, risk appetites, and timescales among parties. Solution: Devise goal congruence.
  • **Approaches to Performance Factors:** Different attitudes towards quality, control, risk. Solution: Devise a common minimum program.
  • **Assigning Accountability:** Key issue due to varied resource, skill, and knowledge contributions. Solution: Clearly establish and communicate accountability.
  • **Lack of Trust:** Parties hesitant to share information. Solution: Mutually decide control/reporting framework, foster trust with compatible management style.
  • **Cultural Conflicts:** Result in poor performance. Solution: Redefine shared values to be more liberal.

Specific Issues in Complex Structures:

  • **Strategic Alliance:** Difficult to implement common performance measures due to retained independence and confidential information concerns.
  • **Joint Venture:** Challenges in assigning accountability due to unequal distribution of resources/work in a separate entity.
  • **Multinationals:** Coordination difficulties due to diverse cultures, languages, time zones, legal/reporting frameworks, and currency fluctuations.
  • **Complex Supply Chain:** Logistical communication barriers, incompatible technology, pressure, lack of trust. Solution: Collaboration and free flow of information.
  • **Virtual Organizations:** Difficulty collecting reliable performance data from widespread sources with limited control.

Information Technology breakthroughs (e.g., uniform shared systems) can be a game-changer for managing performance in complex business structures by ensuring accurate, reliable, and timely information.

Behavioral Aspects of Performance Management

Human behavior significantly impacts organizational performance. Understanding behavioral aspects is crucial for effective performance management systems.

1. Accountability:

When individuals are accountable, they strive for better performance. Accountability for managers (agents of shareholders) is instilled via agency theory; for others, through accounting and management control systems.

Categories of Accountability:

  • **Hard Accountability:** Financial and quantitative information (converting outcomes into numbers, reporting reasons).
  • **Soft Accountability:** Human input in shaping, evaluating, and implementing goals.

Control Mechanisms for Accountability:

  • **Behavioral Control:** Ensures desired actions.
  • **Personnel and Cultural Control:** Provides skilled people and conducive environment.
  • **Reporting Control:** Ensures fair reporting of performance information.

2. Performance Measures as Stimuli:

Clearly communicated performance measures (CSFs & KPIs) act as stimuli, influencing behavior and performance. "What gets measured, gets done" â€“ people exert greater effort in areas where performance is measured.

3. Management Styles & Culture:

Management style must be appropriate to organizational context (e.g., budget-constrained for maturity phase, profit-conscious for growth phase). Overall organizational culture must align with strategy (e.g., Financial Control Cultures for cost leadership, Excellence/Service Cultures for differentiation).

Predicting and Preventing Corporate Failure

Corporate failure is a reflection of deep-seated shortcomings, often due to failure to innovate, hostile environments, too many changes, financial misappropriations, power tussles, or failed strategies. Predicting failure early is strategically significant for corrective and preventive measures.

Models to Predict Corporate Failure:

  • **Quantitative Models:** Compute scores or ranks based on financial ratios (gearing, profitability, liquidity, solvency) to classify companies as surviving or failing. Rely on monetary information.
  • **Qualitative Models:** Assign scores to particular risk factors based on non-financial information (chairperson's address, directors' report, brand equity).

1. Quantitative Models:

Altman Z-Score (1968) - Publicly Held Manufacturing Firms:

Predicts bankruptcy probability within two years. Combines five key ratios into a single discriminant score. Developed by Edward I. Altman.

Altman Z-Score Formula:

$\text{Z Score} = 1.2X_1 + 1.4X_2 + 3.3X_3 + 0.6X_4 + 1.0X_5$

Where:

  • $X_1 = \text{working capital / total assets}$
  • $X_2 = \text{retained earnings / total assets}$
  • $X_3 = \text{earnings before interest and tax / total assets}$
  • $X_4 = \text{market value of equity / total liabilities}$
  • $X_5 = \text{sales / total assets}$

Interpretation of Z-Score (Publicly Held Manufacturing):

  • Less than 1.81: Distress (heading towards bankruptcy).
  • 1.81 to 2.99: Grey (needs further investigation).
  • More than 2.99: Safe (financially sound).

Other Quantitative Models:

  • **Beaver's Univariate Model (1966):** First to use statistical method (t-tests) to predict bankruptcy, but only one ratio at a time.
  • **Taffler and Tishaw's Model (1977):** UK version of Z-score, combining four ratios.
  • **The ZETA Model (1977):** Improved Altman Z-score, incorporating comprehensive inputs.
  • **Altman Z-Score for Private Firms (1983):** Substitutes book value of equity for market value in X4.
  • **Altman Z-Score for Non-manufacturing firms/Emerging Markets (1983):** Revised with four factors to minimize industry effect.
  • **Performance Analysis Score (PAS or Tafflers' PAS):** Relative measure using Z-score in percentile terms.
  • **H Score Model:** Similar to Taffler's PAS, ranked percentile score (0-100), warning area below 25.

Shortcomings of Quantitative Models:

  • Based on financial numbers, can be manipulated.
  • Short-term orientation, relies on historical data.
  • Snapshot view, doesn't highlight root cause or solution.
  • Uses pre-defined weights, reducing reliability.

2. Qualitative Models:

Rely on non-financial information (chairperson's address, directors' report, brand equity). Most prevalent is Argenti's A score model.

Argenti's A Score:

Suggests failure follows a predictable sequence: defects turn into mistakes, leading to symptoms of failure. Has three dimensions/groups: Defects, Mistakes, and Symptoms of Failure.

  • **Defects:** Management weaknesses (e.g., autocratic CEO) and accounting deficiencies (e.g., no budgetary control).
  • **Mistakes:** Occur from defects (e.g., high gearing, overtrading, big project failure).
  • **Symptoms of Failure:** Visible if mistakes continue (e.g., deteriorating ratios, creative accounting).

If total score > 25, company is likely to fail. Group scores reveal root causes.

Shortcomings of Qualitative Models:

  • Subjectivity (expert judgment).
  • Requires large amount of non-financial information, difficult to obtain.
  • Results only as good as inputs; reliability of information is critical.
  • Identifies root-cause but doesn't suggest solutions.

Preventing Corporate Failure:

If predicted, corporate failure can be prevented by early warning signs and quick corrective action (performance improvement strategies).

  • Investigate and identify causes (seek external advice).
  • Managers must accept problems and focus on solutions, not blame.
  • Actions depend on situation (major strategic change, operational changes).
  • Effective management systems are essential to prevent further loss.
